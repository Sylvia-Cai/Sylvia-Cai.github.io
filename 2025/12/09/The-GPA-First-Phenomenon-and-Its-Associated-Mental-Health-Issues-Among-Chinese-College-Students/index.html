The 'GPA First' Phenomenon and Its Associated Mental Health Issues Among Chinese College Students

Posted on 2025-12-09

In China, intense academic pressure persists from high school through university, creating a relentless cycle of studying that extends late into the night. The following scenes capture this reality at two different stages of education.

Photographed at my high school in China: after evening self-study ends at 9:30 PM, everyone returns to their dorm rooms, turns on their desk lamps, and studies until past 11 o’clock.

Photographed at a university in China (Sichuan University): at 9:30 PM, all the classroom buildings are still brightly lit, and students’ bicycles fill the parking lots below.

In China, scenes like this are becoming the norm, from high school to university. In high school, the goal was clear: get into a good university. In university, the goal seems to naturally shift to achieving a high GPA.

I’ve observed that at both top domestic universities and prestigious overseas institutions, many Chinese international students see GPA as their most important, sometimes even sole, “KPI.” It’s common to see friends suffering from constant anxiety or periodic breakdowns because of it.

The Mental Health Crisis

According to the “2024 Report on the Mental Health Status of University Students” (2025), a survey conducted by the National Mental Health Assessment, which involved 60,782 university students, mental health issues among Chinese university students are becoming increasingly prominent. 32.7% of students reported mild or higher psychological distress, with 4.1% reaching a moderate-to-severe level requiring professional intervention. Academic pressure is a leading cause, cited by 69.4% of students as a main source of anxiety, second only to job-hunting stress.

This trend is also visible online. Communities like the Douban group “Mutual Aid Society for the ‘Useless’ Elite Graduates” (2025) have formed, where high-achievers share stories of exhaustion and self-doubt after years of chasing grades. Research supports this observation. Li (2023) points out that the intense focus on academic performance is a major environmental stressor for young people in educational systems.

Why the GPA Fixation? External and Internal Reasons

External Reasons

  1. Systemic Gatekeeping
    Whether applying for graduate school, jobs, or study abroad programs, a high GPA is often the first filter. Xu and Guo (2020) note that at China’s top universities, “performance is king”—grades become the primary currency for future opportunities. This gatekeeping creates a system where students optimize for metrics rather than meaningful learning.

  2. Peer Competition
    When everyone talks about “easy-A” classes and compares GPAs, a competitive atmosphere takes over. Learning shifts from gaining knowledge to winning a race. This environment normalizes comparison and makes collaborative learning feel like a risk to one’s standing.

  3. Family Expectations
    Many parents continue to focus on grades and rankings, just as they did in high school. Though well-intentioned, their questions can make students feel they are studying for their family’s approval, not for themselves. This external validation can overshadow the development of internal motivation and personal academic interests.

Internal Reasons

  1. Familiar Paths
    From elementary through high school, success meant getting high scores. At university, it’s easy to fall back on that familiar way of thinking. Students default to treating university as another series of high-stakes assessments to be maximized, rather than re-evaluating their fundamental approach to learning in a new educational context.

  2. Seeking Control
    The future is uncertain—job markets change, personal goals shift. GPA feels like something you can control and improve in the short term, offering a sense of security. Focusing on improving this one metric provides a manageable goal and a predictable feedback loop, offering a psychological counterweight to broader anxieties about an unpredictable future.

  3. Self-Worth Tied to Grades
    Many of us have learned to equate grades with personal value. A low grade doesn’t just feel like a setback—it can trigger deep self-doubt. This conditioning creates a direct cognitive link between academic performance and core identity, meaning that any critique of a student’s work is often experienced as a critique of the student’s inherent value.

Student Voices: The Human Cost

Student A (Miranda, Economics & Math, UW-Madison, GPA 4.0)
“I have to make every homework assignment perfect, even if it takes much more time than necessary. I know it’s a bit compulsive, but maintaining a 4.0 gives me a sense of ‘order.’ I’m afraid that if I slack off, cracks will appear in this perfect record.”

Student B (Adam, Computer Science, UW-Madison, GPA 3.9)
“My parents always talk about how much they’ve sacrificed and how much money they’ve spent on my education, so subconsciously I feel I must repay them with my grades. They don’t really understand my major, but they ask about my scores every semester. I feel like I’m not just taking exams; I’m fulfilling their expectations.”

Student C (Anny, Domestic Humanities & Social Sciences major)
“During exam weeks, I get extremely anxious, even to the point of heart palpitations. Even though I’ve studied, I’m still afraid others know more than I do. Seeing the library packed makes me even more panicked. Insomnia is routine. Sometimes the pressure gets so overwhelming I just want to run away.”

Student D (Michael, Domestic Computer Science major)
“To get a high GPA, I always choose those supposedly ‘easy-A’ courses instead of the ones I’m genuinely interested in. My GPA looks good now, but I feel lost. I don’t know what I like or what I can do in the future.”

The International Student Experience

For Chinese international students, these pressures are magnified through a cultural and linguistic prism. Beyond navigating rigorous academic demands, they confront what acculturation psychologists term “cumulative stress”: language barriers that make nuanced expression difficult in essays and discussions; social isolation in foreign environments without familiar support networks; and navigating educational systems with different pedagogical assumptions and assessment methods (Koo et al., 2021).

This experience represents what cross-cultural researchers call “double marginalization”—feeling disconnected from both home and host cultures. The intense focus on grades becomes a coping mechanism, offering a concrete goal in an otherwise disorienting experience. As Kipnis (2011) explains, in China, educational desire is often “governed” by powerful social and institutional forces that channel aspirations toward narrow, measurable success. International students carry these cultural scripts abroad, where they often clash with Western educational values emphasizing critical thinking over rote learning.

What We’re Losing in This Pursuit

1. Perspective on Normal University Life

When everyone is stressed about grades, anxiety and sleepless nights become accepted as “just part of the journey.” We rarely question whether such widespread suffering is truly necessary for learning. We lose perspective on what constitutes healthy learning, sacrificing mental wellbeing for academic achievement. This makes students become passive recipients of stress rather than active agents in their education.

2. The True Nature of Learning

Learning transforms from a journey of intellectual discovery into a transactional calculation. Students learn to ask not “What questions does this raise?” but “Will this be on the exam?” This represents a fundamental corruption of education’s purpose, transforming it from a journey of intellectual exploration into a series of transactions. The focus shifts from deep engagement with material to surface-level performance, conditioning students to view knowledge as currency rather than curiosity, actively discouraging exploration beyond examination boundaries.

3. Connection to Self

In the exhausting rush to meet external standards—grades, rankings, and resumes—we silence our inner voice. We stop asking:Who am I? What do I care about? What kind of life do I want to build? The consequences extend far beyond university: adults who haven’t developed strong internal compasses struggle with life decisions, career satisfaction, and meaningful relationships. Student D’s profound sense of being lost exemplifies this developmental arrest.

Redefining Success: The True Purpose of University

University should be a place to:

  1. Explore—Take courses outside your major, attend unfamiliar lectures, and follow curiosity without immediate utility. This is where unexpected interests emerge and intellectual horizons expand.
  2. Discover yourself—Through mentorship, academic challenges, and honest reflection, distinguish between external expectations and what you genuinely value. University shapes not just what you know, but who you become.
  3. Develop durable skills—Cultivate critical thinking to navigate ambiguity, clear communication to convey complex ideas, and independent problem-solving to adapt and persevere. These foundational capacities outlast any exam and enable meaningful contribution in any field.

These capacities do not come with a letter grade, but they become the invisible architecture of a resilient and adaptable mind. They are what enable you to navigate career shifts, contribute to teams, learn new fields, and engage with the world thoughtfully long after any specific exam is forgotten.

Conclusion

A GPA may reflect how well you perform within an academic system, but it should never become the sole measure of your education—or your worth. The library lights will go out each night, but the curiosity and self-awareness you nurture should not. Sometimes, growth begins not by chasing a higher grade, but by stepping back: reading beyond the syllabus, joining a club without resume value, or simply reflecting on what you truly want to learn. That is how university becomes more than a transcript—it becomes a foundation for a thoughtful and purposeful life.
